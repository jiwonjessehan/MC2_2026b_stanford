\section{Scientific Justification}

The Large and Small Magellanic Clouds (LMC and SMC) are the Milky Way's most prominent satellite galaxies, offering an ``outside-in'' view of galactic structure and evolution that is inaccessible for our own Galaxy. Furthermore, their proximity make them key laboratories for studying resolved stellar populations, star formation histories, and dynamical processes of galaxies. While extensive imaging and astrometric surveys have revolutionized our view of the Clouds, spectroscopic coverage remains sparse. Previous large spectroscopic efforts have primarily targeted the brightest and most massive stars with limited spatial sampling. Multi-epoch, contiguous spectroscopic surveys capable of resolving stellar motions across a wide range of stellar types are still largely absent.

\noindent LLAMAS will fundamentally change this landscape. Leveraging Magellan’s large mirror and LLAMAS’s exceptional throughput across the entire optical wavelengths, we will extend spectroscopic monitoring to far fainter stars ($r\sim21$, roughly the main sequence turn-off), achieving much higher target densities across the LMC and SMC than any previous survey. 10-minute exposures, repeated over multiple epochs, will allow us to identify radial velocity variables — including black hole / neutron star binaries and other exotic systems — with high precision. LLAMAS will also open a new regime for mapping the kinematics of the Clouds through their ordinary stellar populations, not just their most luminous members.

\noindent We thus propose \textsc{\textsf{(MC)$^2$}}: the Magellanic Clouds Magellan Campaign. \textsc{\textsf{(MC)$^2$}} will tile the Clouds with LLAMAS, which will yield: (1) a catalog of radial velocity variables, (2) a deep, spatially resolved kinematic map of the LMC Center, and (3) emission line maps of the diffuse interstellar medium. \textsc{\textsf{(MC)$^2$}} is a multi-institutional effort within the Magellan consortium, comprising of Stanford (Jesse Han, Erin Kado-Fong, Marta Nowotka, Susan Clark), CfA (Sophia Ridolfo, Lisa Kewley), and MIT (Kevin Burdge, Emma Chickles).

\section{Observing Plan}

The initial campaign will contiguously tile a $6' \times 6'$ region $(90\text{ pc} \times 90\text{ pc}$ at 50 kpc) centered on the carbon star kinematic center at $(\mathrm{RA}, \mathrm{Dec.}) = (80.90^\circ,\, -68.74^\circ)$ \citep{wan2020}. We will use 10-minute exposures at each pointing. The LLAMAS field of view is $35" \times 34"$, and we adopt a fixed step size of $30"$, with $>4"$ overlap between adjacent fields to facilitate seamless tiling and calibration. Covering the full $6' \times 6'$ area requires 144 pointings, corresponding to 24 hours of exposure per epoch. We will obtain 6 such epochs, yielding a total of 144 hours of exposure. Assuming an average of 8 science hours per night (excluding overheads), the program can be completed in 18 nights. Splitting the time equally between Stanford, CfA, and MIT, we require 6 nights per institution. In 2026A, we request 3 nights (2 dark, 1 gray).

\section{Feasibility and justification of requested observing time}

Based on a standard K-star spectrum, the LLAMAS exposure time calculator predicts a signal-to-noise ratio per pixel of $\sim5$ for an $r=21$ mag star in a single 10-minute exposure. Assuming a resolving power of $R = 2000$ and the detection of at least 10 spectral lines, we estimate a radial velocity (RV) precision of $\sim15~\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$ per single exposure. Stacking all six exposures per field improves the RV precision to $<10~\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$. For comparison, \textit{Gaia} proper motion uncertainties for similar stars correspond to transverse velocity errors exceeding $100~\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$. We will closely work with the LLAMAS team---which is based at MIT---in reducing the data. We will use the \texttt{MINESweeper} \citep{cargile2020} code to fit stellar spectra, of which Jesse Han is an expert user.

Given the forecasted radial velocity precision and observing baseline, LLAMAS will be sensitive to radial velocity variations with semi-amplitudes greater than $\sim 50 \, \rm km\,s^{-1}$ and orbital periods shorter than a month. This sensitivity will enable a search for compact object binaries such as high-mass X-ray binaries and their progenitors. Such systems have already been detected in the LMC, most notably VFTS 243, an O star in a 10.4 day orbit around an unseen companion of at least 9 solar masses \citep{shenar2022}. The initial campaign will also include VFTS 243 as a test case. Of course, \textsc{\textsf{(MC)$^2$}} will be able to perform such a search for a much larger number of stars: in the SMASH survey \citep{nidever2017}, there are at least 500 stars per square arcmin at $r<20$ near this region in the LMC, so we can expect around 18,000 stars with reliable radial velocity measurements. In comparison, the VLT-FLAMES LMC survey \citep{vlt_flames} monitored around 900 stars with $V<17$.

In addition to discovering RV variables, the stacked radial velocity maps will allow us to determine the kinematic center of the LMC with unprecedented precision. Recent work by \citet{Han2025} reports strong evidence for a $\sim10^6 M_\odot$ supermassive black hole (SMBH) in the LMC. However, the exact location of the LMC’s dynamical center—and hence the most probable site of the SMBH—remains poorly constrained, due to uncertainties in proper motion measurements and sparse sampling of the internal velocity field. With the high target density and spatial coverage of the \textsc{\textsf{(MC)$^2$}} radial velocity map, we will be able to model the LMC’s rotational field far more accurately, and localize the SMBH candidate to within a few arcseconds. The $1\sigma$ uncetainty on the stellar kinematic center is $6'$ \citep{wan2020}, which sets the minimum size for this pilot survey.

With line sensitivities on par with the SDSS-V Local Volume Mapper and 50 times better spatial resolution, yet another transformative product from \textsc{\textsf{(MC)$^2$}} will be deep, sub-pc (LLAMAS pixel $\simeq$ 0.18 pc, comparable to cloud scales of 0.1-10 pc) maps in key Balmer, [OIII], and [SII] diagnostic lines of the warm ionized medium in a low-mass satellite environment outside of well-studied star-forming complexes. Erin Kado-Fong and Marta Nowotka will use \textsc{\textsf{(MC)$^2$}} to quantify the nature of multiphase ISM mixing and structure via both multiscale structural metrics and sub-pc structural characterization of known filaments and cores in the \textsc{\textsf{(MC)$^2$}} footprint.

We note that as of the proposal deadline, no \textsc{\textsf{(MC)$^2$}} observations have yet been carried out (the first night is November 9th, 2025). However, Emma Chickles and Kevin Burdge have previously obtained precise radial velocity measurements of an AM CVn system using the He II 4686 Å and He II 5411 Å lines in an earlier LLAMAS observation with an almost identical setup (10 m exposures over multiple epochs). This prior success demonstrates the team’s capability to effectively reduce and analyze the forthcoming data.
